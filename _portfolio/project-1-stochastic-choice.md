**Related publication:** [Tervo et al. (2014), *Cell*, 159(1), 21–32](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.cell.2014.08.037)

## Overview

A fundamental puzzle in systems neuroscience is why animals — and people — don't behave identically in repeated, identical situations. Some variability is noise, but much of it may be deliberate: a strategy for exploring alternative options in an uncertain world.

In this project, we investigated the neural basis of behavioral variability in a well-trained decision-making task in rats. Rather than treating variability as an error or artifact, we asked whether it reflects a structured, brain-controlled process.

## Key Findings

- Trial-by-trial variability in rat behavior arises through a **stochastic choice mechanism**, not random motor noise.
- The **anterior cingulate cortex (ACC)** gates this stochasticity: silencing ACC reduces variability, while activating it increases exploration of alternative choices.
- This provides a mechanistic account of how the brain balances **exploitation** of known good options with **exploration** of alternatives — the classic explore/exploit tradeoff.

## Significance

This work established the ACC as a key controller of behavioral variability and exploration, laying the groundwork for understanding how the prefrontal cortex contributes to adaptive, flexible decision-making. It also provided a behavioral and circuit-level framework that shaped subsequent projects in the lab.
