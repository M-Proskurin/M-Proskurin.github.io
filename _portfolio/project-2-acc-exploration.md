**Related publication:** [Tervo, Kuleshova, Manakov, Proskurin et al. (2021), *Neuron*, 109(11), 1876–1887](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.neuron.2021.03.025)

## Overview

Understanding *why* an animal changes its behavior requires identifying the neural circuits that trigger strategy switches. In this project, we asked a causal question: does the anterior cingulate cortex (ACC) merely reflect behavioral state, or does it actively cause animals to explore new strategies?

We addressed this using a combination of chronic electrophysiology, optogenetic manipulation, and a behavioral task in which rats could switch between different strategies to obtain reward.

## Key Findings

- **Optogenetic activation of ACC** during periods of suboptimal performance significantly increased the probability that animals would switch to an alternative strategy.
- **Suppressing ACC** reduced exploration, causing animals to persist with a strategy even when it was no longer working.
- ACC activity **preceded** behavioral switches, consistent with a causal, rather than just correlational, role.
- These effects were specific to strategy-level decisions, not low-level motor choices.

## Methods & Tools

- In vivo optogenetics (channelrhodopsins and halorhodopsins)
- Chronic tetrode recording in freely behaving rats
- Custom behavioral task with multi-strategy structure
- Statistical analysis of strategy-switching dynamics

## Significance

This study provided direct causal evidence that the ACC is a key node for directing cognitive exploration — actively pushing animals out of suboptimal behavioral routines. It bridges circuit-level mechanism with a computational account of adaptive behavior.
